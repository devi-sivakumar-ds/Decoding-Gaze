Decoding Gaze: Where Are You Looking?


Think about everything you do in a day. When you’re wearing smart glasses, where does your focus actually land?

Does your gaze stay in the same place whether you're busy at work or just hanging out? And does everyone look at the world through their lenses in the same way, or is your "visual footprint" unique to you?

Let’s dive in and see what’s really happening behind the lens!

Turns out — what you're doing is linked to where you look.

When you're cooking, your gaze naturally drops toward the bottom of the lens because you're focused on your hands, the cutting board, or the pan. But when you’re relaxing, your focus tends to float toward the top of the lens as you watch TV or look around the room.

To see if this happens for everyone, we can look at the Nymeria dataset, which features 640 recordings from 236 different people. It’s a massive window into how people move their eyes in the real world—so let’s look at the patterns we find!

Where Does Each Activity Land on the Lens?

Each dot is the average eye position on the lens for one activity. The oval around each dot shows the spread — how much different people vary within that activity.

Cooking gaze lands 3.5 mm lower on the lens than relaxing. This difference held for 9 out of 10 people in the study.

But Does This Hold for Everyone?

Each dot is one real recording from one real person. If the pattern were only an average fluke, you'd expect the dots to blur together. Instead, activities tend to sit in different vertical ranges, with overlap. Toggle the checkbox to compare the gender pattern too.

The data comes from the Nymeria Dataset — a collection of 1,100 recordings made by people wearing Project Aria research glasses from Meta. Think of them as scientific smart glasses: they have an outward-facing camera and an eye tracker that captures exactly where you're looking 10 times per second.

We only used sessions with individually-tuned eye calibration — meaning the tracker was adjusted to fit each person's eyes specifically, giving much higher accuracy. That narrowed 1,100 total recordings down to our 640.
